** Notion of weakness
- "Weak" roots have a morphophonological alternation.
- Here I will only talk about roots containing an underlying \textit{ə} vowel.

** Notation
- Root templates are marked in colour only: /CəCVC/.
- I will be talking about reconstructed underlying forms:
  - \textbf{*/tahək/}
  - These are hypothesized URs in Proto-Atayal (PA).

** Why do this?
- I tried to reconcile apparent exceptions in antepenult vowel reflexes.
- Abstracting over root templates led to additional conclusions.

* Finding weak roots
** Distribution of surface [ə]
\textit{ə} has a limited distribution in all Atayal dialects:
- it can never appear in the final syllable,
- it is restricted to initial open syllables in Matu'uwal (Ml),
- it does not appear in surface forms in Plngawan (Pl) and Matu'aw at all.

** Reflexes of *ə
- In the final syllable \textit{*ə} always surfaces as \textit{u}.
- In CVCəCVC forms (including CəCəCVC), Matu'uwal and Plngawan delete the medial \textit{ə} and resyllabify.
- Matu'aw merges all non-final \textit{*ə} into \textit{a}.
- Other dialects preserve penult \textit{*ə} as \textit{ə}.

** Reflexes of *ə
- Some reflexes of non-final *ə in Matu'uwal and Plngawan appear to be in disagreement.
  - e.g. \text{*/ləhug/} 'to thread' → Ml /luhug-un/, Pl /lahug-an/.

* Examples of weak roots
** Types of weak roots
- In Atayal, like many Austronesian languages, the canonical root is /CVCVC/.
- I will mostly focus on canonical roots that contain a schwa in one or both syllables:
  - /CəCVC/, /CVCəC/, and /CəCəC/.

** Finding weak roots
- Penult /ə/ usually apparent in AV (prefixed/infixed) forms.
- Ultimate /ə/ can be distinguished from /u/ only in suffixed forms.

* Findings
** Findings
- Separate patterns for reflexes of suffixed CəCVC roots in Matu'uwal and Plngawan.
- Distributional asymmetry in weak roots at the Proto-Atayal level.
  
* Reflexes in individual dialects

- CəCuC + -an/-un = CuCuC-
- CəCaC + -an/-un = CaCaC-

** CəCVC roots in Plngawan
- Reflex of /*ə/ depends on the syllable structure of suffixed form.
- Some roots have vowel coalescence with the suffix, resulting in /CVCVn/ on the surface.
  - /*ə/ assumes penultimate position.

** Vowel coalescence in Plngawan
\text{/səkuʔ/} + -un = suk\textbf{u}n

* Weak root asymmetry

** Asymmetry
- Plenty of /CəCəC/ and /CəCVC/ roots.
- Almost no /CiCəC/ (only \text{*/qihəl/} 'to force'), and no attested /CuCəC/ --- conspicuous gap!
- Majority of /CaCəC/ inherited from PAn.

** Nominal CVCəC
- /CiCəC/ and /CuCəC/ nominal roots have been inherited from PAn.
- However, these do not alternate, and the /*ə/ became /u/ in Atayal.

* Conclusion
** Reflexes
- Apparent irregularities in reflexes of /*ə/ are due to different patterns in Ml and Pl.
- Sound changes differ in nominal vs verbal roots.
  
** Asymmetry
- Few CVCəC roots, mostly CaCəC inherited from PAn.
- Accidental gap, or dispreference for full vowels to precede /ə/ in the root?
